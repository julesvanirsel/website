Biography

Welcome to my online outline of personal and professional endeavors. My name is Jules van Irsel; I am a space plasma physicist, rocket instrument scientist, and former mechanical design engineer. I am currently working on my Ph.D. dissertation covering electric current connectivity in atmospheres surrounding aurorae, working extensively with 3D plasma fluid simulations.

Some of my skills include mechanical and electrical computer aided design, engineering, manufacturing, and testing, as well as laboratory plasma testing for rocket instruments measuring ion flow. I am also proficient in the core branches of physics, multilingual computational physics, and large data management.

Fundamentally, I have endless intellectual curiosity which enables me to have diverse strengths. I am curious, conscientious, and interdisciplinary.

Thank you for your interest in my work!

Professional Highlights:

Multi-Fluid Plasma Modeling

Throughout my Ph.D. I have worked extensively with state-of-the-art 3D ionospheric simulations provided by the Geospace Environment Model of Ion-Neutral Interactions (GEMINI).

The GEMINI model, developed in large part by Matt Zettergren, Joshua Semeter, and Jonathan Snively, is a multi-fluid, quasi-electrostatic model which keeps track of the various state parameters of six different ion species as well as electrons. Find out more about my own contributions to this model!

NASA MIDEX Proposal

I have provided support on proposals for several NASA funding solicitations, including the 2019 Heliophysics Medium-Class Explorer proposal named the Auroral Reconstruction CubeSwarm (ARCS).

Through an array of 32 CubeSats (red) and many ground-based, all-sky observatories spanning most of Alaska (green), this proposed mission aims to uncover the physics behind temporal and spatial gradients pertaining to auroral ionospheric plasma. Such gradients are impossible to measure with 1-dimensional data tracks provided by singular spacecraft.

My contributions to this work included providing a catalog Observing System Simulation Experiments (OSSE), using GEMINI, to ensure proper measurement tolerances and redundancies.
