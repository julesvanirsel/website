Biography

Welcome to my online outline of personal and professional endeavors. My name is Jules van Irsel; I am a space plasma physicist, rocket instrument scientist, and former mechanical design engineer. I am currently working on my Ph.D. dissertation covering electric current connectivity in atmospheres surrounding aurorae, working extensively with 3D plasma fluid simulations.

Some of my skills include mechanical and electrical computer aided design, engineering, manufacturing, and testing, as well as laboratory plasma testing for rocket instruments measuring ion flow. I am also proficient in the core branches of physics, multilingual computational physics, and large data management.

Fundamentally, I have endless intellectual curiosity which enables me to have diverse strengths. I am curious, conscientious, and interdisciplinary.

Thank you for your interest in my work!

Professional Highlights:

Multi-Fluid Plasma Modeling

Throughout my Ph.D. program, I have worked extensively with state-of-the-art 3D ionospheric simulations provided by the Geospace Environment Model of Ion-Neutral Interactions, or GEMINI, using the NASA Advanced Supercomputing (NAS) Division at Ames Research Center.

In brief, GEMINI was first created by Matt Zettergren, Joshua Semeter, and Jonathan Snively, and is continuously evolving. It is a multi-fluid, quasi-electrostatic model which keeps track of the various state parameters, such as pressure and temperature, of electrons and six different ion species as they succumb to electromagnetic, gravitational, and fluid forces. Find out more about my own contributions to this model!

A large part of my research has been funded by NASA's Future Investigators in NASA Earth and Space Science and Technology (FINESST) 2022 solicitation. Feel free to have a look at my proposal!

NASA MIDEX Proposal

I have provided support on proposals for several NASA funding solicitations, including the 2019 Heliophysics Medium-Class Explorer proposal named the Auroral Reconstruction CubeSwarm (ARCS).

Through an array of 32 CubeSats (as shown here in red) and many ground-based, all-sky observatories spanning most of Alaska (green), this proposed mission aims to uncover the physics behind temporal and spatial gradients pertaining to auroral ionospheric plasma. Such gradients are impossible to measure with 1-dimensional data tracks provided by singular spacecraft.

My contributions to this work includes providing a catalog Observing System Simulation Experiments (OSSE), using GEMINI, to ensure specific measurement tolerances and proper redundancies.

Find out more about the ARCS, my work on the 2022 Heliophysics Low Cost Access to Space (HLCAS) proposal, or my own NASA FINESST proposal for graduate funding!

Sounding Rockets

In addition to my research, I also do testing and field work providing support for sounding rocket instruments. Two such instruments I have worked with are the Miniature Plasma Imager (MPI) developed by Johnathan Burchill et al., and Petite Ion Probes (PIP) developed by Kristina Lynch et al.

This support includes electronics, hardware, vibration, ultra-high vacuum, plasma, and general system testing, as well as integration of these instrument systems into various sounding rocket payloads.
